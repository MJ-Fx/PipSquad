The Global Marketplace

Financial markets are the lifeblood of the global economy, facilitating the exchange of capital and credit through various instruments. These markets can be categorized into several types, each with unique characteristics, participants, and trading mechanisms.

1. The Forex Market

Market Structure

Decentralized Over-the-Counter (OTC) market with no physical location or central exchange. Operates through an electronic network of banks, brokers, and traders worldwide.

Trading Hours

Open 24 hours a day, 5 days a week across major financial centers: Sydney, Tokyo, London, and New York.

Liquidity

The most liquid market globally, with the US dollar involved in 85% of all transactions.

Currency Pair Categories

Major Pairs

  • EUR/USD (Euro/US Dollar)
  • USD/JPY (US Dollar/Japanese Yen)
  • GBP/USD (British Pound/US Dollar)
  • USD/CHF (US Dollar/Swiss Franc)
  • AUD/USD (Australian Dollar/US Dollar)
  • USD/CAD (US Dollar/Canadian Dollar)
  • NZD/USD (New Zealand Dollar/US Dollar)

Minor Pairs

  • EUR/GBP (Euro/British Pound)
  • EUR/JPY (Euro/Japanese Yen)
  • GBP/JPY (British Pound/Japanese Yen)
  • AUD/JPY (Australian Dollar/Japanese Yen)
  • EUR/AUD (Euro/Australian Dollar)

Exotic Pairs

  • USD/SGD (US Dollar/Singapore Dollar)
  • USD/HKD (US Dollar/Hong Kong Dollar)
  • USD/TRY (US Dollar/Turkish Lira)
  • EUR/TRY (Euro/Turkish Lira)
  • USD/ZAR (US Dollar/South African Rand)

2. Stock Markets

Stock markets facilitate the buying and selling of shares in publicly traded companies. Unlike forex, stocks trade on centralized exchanges during specific hours.

New York Stock Exchange (NYSE)

Hours: 9:30AM - 4:00PM EST

Notable Companies: Apple, Coca-Cola, Walmart

NASDAQ

Hours: 9:30AM - 4:00PM EST

Notable Companies: Amazon, Google, Tesla

London Stock Exchange (LSE)

Hours: 8:00AM - 4:30PM GMT

Notable Companies: BP, GlaxoSmithKline, HSBC

Tokyo Stock Exchange (TSE)

Hours: 9:00AM - 3:00PM JST

Notable Companies: Toyota, Sony, Mitsubishi

Types of Stocks

Blue Chips

Large, established companies with stable earnings (e.g., Apple, Microsoft)

Growth Stocks

Companies expected to grow faster than market average (e.g., Tesla, Nvidia)

Dividend Stocks

Companies that regularly pay dividends (e.g., Coca-Cola, Procter & Gamble)

Penny Stocks

Small companies with shares under $5, higher risk/reward

3. Indices Market

Indices measure the performance of a group of stocks representing a particular market or sector. They provide a snapshot of market health and are popular trading instruments.

Major Global Indices

Price-Weighted Blue Chips

500 large-cap US companies

NAS100 (NASDAQ 100)

100 largest non-financial NASDAQ companies

Tech Heavy Growth Focus

FTSE 100 (UK100)

100 largest LSE companies by market cap

International Exposure Commodity Influence

DAX (GER40)

40 major German companies

Performance Index Eurozone Benchmark

Nikkei 225 (JPN225)

225 large Japanese companies

Price-Weighted Export Focus

Synthetic Indices

Special indices that mimic real-world market movements but are generated by random number algorithms with constant volatility.

Volatility Indices

Simulate volatile market conditions with rapid price movements

Boom & Crash

Feature dramatic spikes and drops in short timeframes

Move in predetermined increments with periodic jumps

Jump Indices

Experience sudden price jumps at random intervals

6. Market Correlations

Financial markets are interconnected, with movements in one asset class often influencing others. Understanding these relationships can enhance trading strategies.

USD & Commodities

Most commodities are priced in USD, creating an inverse relationship. When USD strengthens, commodities typically become more expensive in other currencies, reducing demand.

Gold & Safe Havens

Gold often moves inversely to stocks and positively with other safe havens like JPY and CHF during market turmoil.

Oil & CAD

Canada is a major oil exporter, making CAD positively correlated with crude oil prices.

Cryptos & Tech Stocks

Cryptocurrencies often follow similar patterns to tech stocks (NASDAQ) as both are considered risk assets.

Bonds & Stocks

Typically inverse relationship - when bond prices rise (yields fall), stocks often decline as investors seek safety.

AUD & China

Australia's economy is tied to Chinese demand for commodities, making AUD sensitive to China's economic health.
