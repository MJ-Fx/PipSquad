Understanding Fundamental Analysis

Fundamental analysis is the process of examining economic, financial, and other qualitative and quantitative factors to evaluate a security's intrinsic value. Unlike technical analysis which focuses on price movements, fundamental analysis looks at the underlying factors that affect a company's business and future prospects.

Technical Analysis

  • Studies price movements
  • Uses charts and indicators
  • Short-term focus
  • Based on market psychology

Fundamental Analysis

  • Studies economic/company data
  • Uses financial statements
  • Long-term focus
  • Based on intrinsic value

Core Principle

"Fundamental analysts believe that markets may misprice securities in the short run but will eventually recognize and correct to their 'fair value' based on fundamental factors in the long run."

Central Bank Policies

Central banks like the Federal Reserve, ECB, and BOJ significantly influence markets through monetary policy:

Quantitative Easing (QE)

Central bank purchases of securities to increase money supply and encourage lending.

Market Impact: Generally bullish for stocks

Interest Rate Hikes

Increasing rates to combat inflation slows economic growth.

Market Impact: Generally bearish for stocks

Forward Guidance

Communication about future policy intentions influences market expectations.

Market Impact: Depends on guidance

Economic Cycle Analysis

Economies move through predictable cycles that affect different sectors:

Expansion

GDP growing, employment rising

Best Sectors: Tech, Consumer Discretionary

Peak

Growth slowing, inflation rising

Best Sectors: Energy, Materials

Contraction

GDP shrinking, unemployment rising

Best Sectors: Utilities, Consumer Staples

Trough

Economy bottoming, recovery begins

Best Sectors: Financials, Industrials

Industry Analysis

After analyzing the broader economy, fundamental analysts examine specific industries to identify those with favorable growth prospects and competitive dynamics.

Industry Analysis Methods

Porter's Five Forces

  1. Competitive rivalry
  2. Supplier power
  3. Buyer power
  4. Threat of substitution
  5. Threat of new entry

SWOT Analysis

  • Strengths: Internal advantages
  • Weaknesses: Internal disadvantages
  • Opportunities: External favorable factors
  • Threats: External challenges

Industry Life Cycle

  • Introduction phase
  • Growth phase
  • Maturity phase
  • Decline phase

Key Industry Metrics

Total Addressable Market (TAM)

The total revenue opportunity available for a product or service.

Growth Rate

Year-over-year revenue growth for the industry.

Profit Margins

Average net profit margins across industry participants.

Barriers to Entry

Obstacles that prevent new competitors from entering.

Valuation Methods

Comparable Company Analysis

Values company relative to similar public companies.

Steps:

  1. Select comparable companies
  2. Calculate valuation multiples
  3. Apply multiples to target company
  4. Determine valuation range

Pros

  • Market-based
  • Simple to understand
  • Uses current data

Cons

  • Hard to find perfect comps
  • Market may be mispriced
  • Ignores company specifics

Precedent Transactions

Values company based on prices paid for similar companies in M&A.

Steps:

  1. Identify comparable transactions
  2. Calculate transaction multiples
  3. Apply multiples to target company
  4. Include control premium

Pros

  • Real transaction prices
  • Includes control premium
  • Industry-specific

Cons

  • Limited data available
  • Deals may be outdated
  • Strategic premiums distort

Sum of the Parts

Values each business segment separately then sums them up.

Steps:

  1. Break company into segments
  2. Value each segment appropriately
  3. Sum segment valuations
  4. Add/subtract corporate items

Pros

  • Good for conglomerates
  • Identifies hidden value
  • Segment-specific multiples

Cons

  • Complex for large companies
  • Allocation issues
  • Inter-segment transactions

Asset-Based Valuation

Values company based on its net asset value.

Value = (Total Assets - Total Liabilities) √∑ Shares Outstanding

Pros

  • Tangible valuation
  • Good for asset-heavy firms
  • Conservative approach

Cons

  • Ignores earnings power
  • Assets may be over/undervalued
  • Not for service firms

Apple Inc. (AAPL)

Sector: Technology Industry: Consumer Electronics Founded: 1976

Economic Analysis

Apple operates in a growing global smartphone and personal computing market, though faces cyclical demand and currency headwinds in international markets.

Industry Analysis

The smartphone industry is mature with high competition, but Apple maintains premium pricing power through its ecosystem and brand loyalty.

Financial Analysis

Revenue Growth (YoY) +8.1%
Gross Margin 42.3%
Net Margin 24.6%
ROE 147.3%
Current Ratio 0.9
Debt-to-Equity 1.7

Valuation

P/B Ratio 38.2
EV/EBITDA 21.4

Investment Thesis

Apple's strong brand, ecosystem, and services growth justify premium valuation, though high dependence on iPhone sales and supply chain risks remain concerns.
